Faisal Mosque

The Shah Faisal Mosque in Islamabad, completed in 1986 and designed by the renowned Turkish architect Vedat Dalokay, is a world-famous masterwork of contemporary Islamic architecture that serves as an iconic symbol of Pakistan. Situated at the northernmost edge of the city against the picturesque backdrop of the Margalla Hills, the mosque was funded as a gift from King Faisal bin Abdulaziz of Saudi Arabia and can accommodate over 300,000 worshippers across its main hall, courtyards, and grounds. Moving away from traditional domes, Dalokay designed the main structure to resemble a concrete Bedouin desert tent, featuring an eight-sided shell shaped by sharp, intersecting triangles that ascend into an internal ceiling reaching 134 feet high. The structure is anchored by four slender, pencil-shaped Turkish minarets that tower 260 feet into the air—making them the tallest minarets in South Asia—while the interior is decorated with pure white marble, intricate mosaics, and a massive golden chandelier designed by Pakistani artist Gulgee.
